Marco Montanari

Dal punto di vista letterario, sicuramente Issac Asimoc incarna un punto di vista disincantato e critico, ma pur sempre positivo verso le evoluzioni tecnologiche, anche se a volte sembra di scorgere venature di ironia e sarcasmo nei suoi racconti.

Un esempio di questo pensiero è l'introduzione a "I, Robot".

Introduction

I LOOKED AT MY NOTES AND I DIDN'T LIKE THEM. I'd spent three days at U. S. Robots and might as well have spent them at home with the Encyclopedia Tellurica.
Susan Calvin had been born in the year 1982, they said, which made her seventy-five now. Everyone knew that. Appropriately enough, U. S. Robot and Mechanical Men, Inc. was seventy-five also, since it had been in the year of Dr. Calvin's birth that Lawrence Robertson had first taken out incorporation papers for what eventually became the strangest industrial giant in man's history. Well, everyone knew that, too.
At the age of twenty, Susan Calvin had been part of the particular Psycho-Math seminar at which Dr. Alfred Lanning of U. S. Robots had demonstrated the first mobile robot to be equipped with a voice. It was a large, clumsy unbeautiful robot, smelling of machine-oil and destined for the projected mines on Mercury. But it could speak and make sense.
Susan said nothing at that seminar; took no part in the hectic discussion period that followed. She was a frosty girl, plain and colorless, who protected herself against a world she disliked by a masklike expression and a hypertrophy of intellect. But as she watched and listened, she felt the stirrings of a cold enthusiasm.
She obtained her bachelor's degree at Columbia in 2003 and began graduate work in cybernetics.
All that had been done in the mid-twentieth century on "calculating machines" had been upset by Robertson and his positronic brain-paths. The miles of relays and photocells had given way to the spongy globe of plantinumiridium about the size of a human brain.
She learned to calculate the parameters necessary to fix the possible variables within the "positronic brain"; to construct "brains" on paper such that the responses to given stimuli could be accurately predicted.
In 2008, she obtained her Ph.D. and joined United States Robots as a "Robopsychologist," becoming the first great practitioner of a new science. Lawrence Robertson was still president of the corporation; Alfred Lanning had become director of research.
For fifty years, she watched the direction of human progress change and leap ahead.
Now she was retiring - as much as she ever could. At least, she was allowing someone else's name to be inset upon the door of her office.
That, essentially, was what I had. I had a long list of her published papers, of the patents in her name; I had the chronological details of her promotions. In short I had her professional "vita" in full detail.
But that wasn't what I wanted.
I needed more than that for my feature articles for Interplanetary Press. Much more.
I told her so.
"Dr. Calvin," I said, as lushly as possible, "in the mind of the public you and U. S. Robots are identical. Your retirement will end an era and-"
"You want the human-interest angle?" She didn't smile at me. I don't think she ever smiles. But her eyes were sharp, though not angry. I felt her glance slide through me and out my occiput and knew that I was uncommonly transparent to her; that everybody was.
But I said, "That's right."
"Human interest out of robots? A contradiction."
"No, doctor. Out of you."
"Well, I've been called a robot myself. Surely, they've told you I'm not human."
They had, but there was no point in saying so.
She got up from her chair. She wasn't tall and she looked frail. I followed her to the window and we looked out.
The offices and factories of U. S. Robots were a small city; spaced and planned. It was flattened out like an aerial photograph.
"When I first came here," she said, "I had a little room in a building right about there where the fire-house is now." She pointed. "It was torn down before you were born. I shared the room with three others. I had half a desk. We built our robots all in one building. Output - three a week. Now look at us."
"Fifty Years," I hackneyed, "is a long time."
"Not when you're looking back at them," she said. "You wonder how they vanished so quickly."
She went back to her desk and sat down. She didn't need expression on her face to look sad, somehow.
"How old are you?" she wanted to know.
"Thirty-two," I said.
"Then you don't remember a world without robots. There was a time when humanity faced the universe alone and without a friend. Now he has creatures to help him; stronger creatures than himself, more faithful, more useful, and absolutely devoted to him. Mankind is no longer alone. Have you ever thought of it that way?"
"I'm afraid I haven't. May I quote you?"
"You may. To you, a robot is a robot. Gears and metal; electricity and positrons. Mind and iron! Human-made! It necessary, human-destroyed! But you haven't worked with them, so you don't know them. They're a cleaner better breed than we are."
I tried to nudge her gently with words, "We'd like to hear some of the things you could tell us; get your views on robots. The Interplanetary Press reaches the entire Solar System. Potential audience is three billion, Dr. Calvin. They ought to know what you could tell them on robots."
It wasn't necessary to nudge. She didn't hear me, but she was moving in the right direction.
"They might have known that from the start. We sold robots for Earth-use then - before my time it was, even. Of course, that was when robots could not talk. Afterward, they became more human and opposition began. The labor unions, of course, naturally opposed robot competition for human jobs, and various segments of religious opinion had their superstitious objections. It was all quite ridiculous and quite useless. And yet there it was."
I was taking it down verbatim on my pocket-recorder, trying not to show the knuckle-motions of my hand. If you practice a bit, you can get to the point where you can record accurately without taking the little gadget out of your pocket.
"Take the case of Robbie," she said. "I never knew him. He was dismantled the year before I joined the company - hopelessly out-of-date. But I saw the little girl in the museum-"
She stopped, but I didn't say anything. I let her eyes mist up and her mind travel back. She had lots of time to cover.
"I heard about it later, and when they called us blasphemers and demon-creators, I always thought of him. Robbie was a non-vocal robot. He couldn't speak. He was made and sold in 1996. Those were the days before extreme specialization, so he was sold as a nursemaid-"
"As a what?"
"As a nursemaid-"

In this introduction we can see part of Asimov's thought on Artificial Intlligence. In fact the last part of the introduction hits the point of modern philosophical theories.

Mankind would never accept an intelligence that is not human, and not even something that makes things like human beings, unless he is sure that it could never "want" to do things.

Asimov tries to avoid to use the AI or the robots in the place left by gnomes and elves of fantastic novels. In fact he uses this stereotype just in a few of them. In the introduction to "The Complete Robot" he writes:

"something odd happened as I wrote this first story. I managed to get the dim vision of a robot as neither Menace nor Pathos. I began to think of robots as industrial products built by matter-of-fact engineers. They were built with safety features so they weren't Menaces and they were fashioned for certain jobs so that no Pathos was necessarily involved.
As I continued to write robot stories, this notion of carefully engineered industrial robots permeated my stories more and more until the whole character of robot stories in serious printed science fiction changed-not only that of my own stories, but of just about everybody's. "

This is a perfect explanation of what Asimov writes. In fact he nearly never writes stories about robots killing people, but stories of people and robots living together peacefully.

This happens in "A Boy's Best friend", in which Asimov tries to show the reader that it is not important to have feelings only towards living creatures. The important thing is to have feelings.

Ths is the perfect explanation of what the differences between Asimov and another great Science Fiction writer: Arthur C. Clarke.

In his main book, "2001, Odissey in space", Hal 9000, a very intelligent computer, kills one by one the astronauts he would have had to cooperate with, because they wanted to shut it down. The story is much more complex, but about AI, it's the important thing

In this book you see perfectly how the machine stands for the Orch of ancient stories (even though in reality man is often more "Orch" than the Orch himself).

In fact a modern civilisation like ours, would no longer accept gnomes, elves and fairies. Asimov writes about it in

  • Someday

In this novel the writer looks at modern times in a critical, but not negative way. In fact, the real negativity is in the boys, not in the machine. It suffers just a stuation, in which even a human being whoud have felt the same way.

Another interesting subject Asimov faces is the problem of the difference between living beings and the machine. There are two interesting Novels about this problem:

  • A boy's Best Friend
  • The Bicentennial Man

In the first novel, as we already said, the different is not in chemical or phisical aspects, but it's just the man's will to transform a machine into a living being. The boy in the story prefers a robotic dog (it remembers Aibo, by Sony) instead of a real dog, because the robotic one is "his own" dog and it is the one he loves, even though his feelings are programmed. The last paragraph shows perfectly this point of view:

"Jimmy held Robutt tightly. He was frowning, too, and the desperate look on his face meant that he wouldn't change his mind. He said, 'But what's the difference how they act? How about how I feel? I love Robutt and that's what counts.'
And the little robot-mutt, which had never been held so tightly in all its existence, squeaked high and rapid squeaks-happy squeaks."

The second novel is about a robot who is given creativity and will, two very human features. In fact, one day he asks his owner to give him freedom. After some legal problems (the fact that a "thing" can't own itself), he begins a long way of analysys and studies, that brigs him to meet the son of his creator, and with him they begin to "humanise" Andrew. He gains a skin, blood circulation, a nervous system. all the projects for these organs are made by Andrew, rhe Robot. After 175 years, Andrew, now part of the factory, decides not to be only a human being de facto, but even de lege. U.S. Robots begins a long legal campaign, that will create all the precedents and will bring Andrew's case to the World Parliament.

The pessimistic views don't stop Andrew, who takes at the end the ending decision: he creates degenerative processes in his own neural-positronic brain.

During the party for his 200th birthday the president of the World Parliament declares him officially a human being.

The same afternoon Andrew dies surrounded by all persons who were important to him.

In the novel there are some very interesting ... about mmacine and man:

"It all comes down to the brain, then," Andrew said cautiously. "But must we leave it at the level of cells versus positrons? Is there no way of forcing a functional definition? Must we say that a brain is made of this or that? May we not say that a brain is something- anything- capable of a certain level of thought?"

As we can see, it is the great question of functionalism Turing asks when creating the finite state machine. And it is the very definition of Functionalism.

Moreover:

"Isn't that the fundamental barrier: human beings can tolerate an immortal robot, for it doesn't matter how long a machine lasts, but they cannot tolerate an immortal human being since their own mortality is endurable only so long as it is universal. "

Men don't accept what they create.

"Won't work," said Li-hsing. "Your brain is manmade, the human brain is not. Your brain is constructed, theirs developed. To any human being who is intent on keeping up the barrier between himself and a robot, those differences are a steel wall a mile high and a mile thick."

In quest'introduzione si nota parte della visione di Asimov sull'Intelligenza Artificiale. Infatti nelle ultime righe si nota tutto l'interesse dell'autore per le moderne teorie filosofiche.

L'umanità non accetterebbe mai un'intelligenza che non sia umana, e nemmeno qualcosa che renda le macchine simili a esser umani, a meno che non ci sia la garanzia che la macchina non possa mai "voler" fare cose.

Asimov cerca, però, sempre di evitare di sostituire gli orchi e gli gnomi delle favole con i robot e i computer intelligenti. Utilizza questo stereotipo solo raramente. Scrive infatti, nell'introduzione a Tutti i miei robot "

"La morale dei racconti sui robot malvagi era che l'uomo non deve mai spingere la sua conoscenza oltre a un certo punto. Ma anche da giovane non riuscivo a condividere l'opinione che, se la conoscenza è pericolosa, la soluzione ideale risieda nell'ignoranza."

Questa è la spiegazione di cosa scrive Asimov. Infatti non scrive quasi mai testi nei quali i robot uccidono esseri umani, ma storie di robot ed esseri umani che vivono pacificamente insieme.

Questo avviene anche in "Il miglior amico dell'uomo", nel quale Asimov mostra al lettore che non è importante avere emozioni solo verso esseri viventi. L'importante è avere emozioni. E questo racconto dimostra anche un'altro fatto, di cui però mi occupoerò dopo.

Dall'evitare l'uso di stereotipi nasce il netto contrasto con le opere di un'altro dei più grandi scrittori di fantascienza: Arthur C. Clarke.

Nella sua opera più diffusa e nota, anche grazie al film, "2001: Odissea nello Spazio", infatti, Hal 9000, computer estremamente intelligente, uccide uno per uno gli astronauti con cui dovrebbe collaborare perchè questi avevano voluto spegnerlo. La storia è in realtà molto più complessa, ma per quanto riguarda l'IA questa è la cosa importante.

Qui si vede perfettamente come la macchina si sostituisca all'Orco delle fiabe (anche se nella realtà l'uomo è più "Orco" dell'Orco stesso).

D'altronde una civiltà modernizzata come la nostra, non potrebbe più accettare gli gnomi, le fate e gli elfi. Questo è testimoniato un altro splendido racconto di Asimov:

  • Un Giorno

In questo i racconto si nota una tendenza dell'autore a vedere la modernità in modo critico, ma mai negativo. Infatti, la vera negatività è quella dei due ragazzi, non della macchina. Questa subisce solo una situazione, nella quale anche un essere umano si sarebbe sentito allo stesso modo.

Un altro argomento che Asimov ha sviluppato con molto interesse e in modo molto "preveggente", è il problema della differenza fra essere vivente e macchina. Per quanto riguarda questo problema, i racconti interesanti sono vari:

  • Il miglior amico dell'Uomo
  • L'uomo bicentenario

Nel primo racconto, come già detto il discriminante non è una caratteristica fisica o chimica. E' semplicemente la volontà dell'uomo a rendere un essere inanimato un essere vivente. Infatti nella storia un cane robotico (ricorda Aibo della Sony...) viene preferito da un bambino ad un cane vero, perchè in realtà per lui il "suo" cane è quello in metallo, anche se i suoi sentimenti sono solo programmati. Infatti l'ultimo paragrafo del racconto estremizza questo punto di vista:

"Jimmy teneva Robutt [così si chiama il robot-cane] stretto. Stava immobile... , e lo sguardo disperato sul suo volto significava che non avrebbe cambiato idea. Disse: ' Ma che differenza c'è riguardo a come agiscono? Non importa a nessuno cosa sento io? Io amo Robutt a questo è quello che conta.'
E il piccolo robot-cane, che non era mai stato tenuto così stretto in tutta la sua esistenza, emise rapidi e acuti squittii - squittii di gioia."

Il secondo racconto, dal quale è stato tratto recentemente un film, tratta invece un robot a cui per sbaglio è stata donata la creatività e la volontà, caratteristiche molto umane. Il robot, infatti, un giorno chiede al suo proprietario che gli sia data la libertà. Nonostante vari problemi legali, questo inizia un lungo percorso di sudi e analisi che lo porta ad incontrare il figlio del suo progettista e ad iniziare insieme a lui il processo di umanizzazione. Riceve infatti una pelle, un circuito sanguigno, un sistema nervoso. Tutti i progetti sono firmati da Andrew, il robot. Dopo centosettatacinque anni, Andrew, ormai parte integrante della ditta, decide di voler diventare non solo un essere umano de facto, ma anche de lege. Infatti inizia una campagna legale che portaerà la U.S. Robots a creare una quantità incredibile di precedenti e porterà il caso "Andrew" al Parlamento Mondiale.

Le previsioni di sconfitta non scoraggiano Andrew, che infine prende la decisione deifinitiva: crea processi degenerativi nel cervello positronico-neurale.

in occasione del suo bicentenario il Presidente del Parlamento Mondiale lo dichiara ufficialmente Essere umano.

Il pomeriggio stesso Andrew si spegne - anzi - muore circondato da tutte le persone più importanti della sua vita.

Nel racconto sono espressi vari concetti molto importanti su macchina e uomo:

"Tutto si riduce insomma al cervello", disse cauto Andrew. "Ma dobbiamo lasciare la differenza al livello cellule contro positroni? Non c'è modo di dare una definizione funzionale? Dobbiamo proprio dire che il cervello è fatto di questo o quello? Non possiamo dire che il cervello è qualcosa - una qualunque cosa - capace di un certo livello di pensiero?"

Questa è, come si nota, la grande domanda funzionalista che Turing si pone quando crea la macchina a stati finiti. Ed è la definizione stessa di Funzionalismo.

E ancora:

"Non funzionerà", disse Li-hsing. "Il tuo cervello è fatto dall'uomo, quello umano no. Il tuo cervello è stato costruito, quello umano si è evoluto. Per ogni essere umano intento a mantenere le distanze dai robot questo è un muro in acciaio lungo un miglio e altrettanto alto.

L'uomo insomma non accetta tutto ciò che lui stesso crea.

"Non è questa la barriera fondamentale: gli esseri umani possono tollerare un robot immortale, perchè non importa quanto dura una macchina, ma non possono tollerare un essere umano immortale poichè la loro stessa mortalità è accettabile in quanto universale"
